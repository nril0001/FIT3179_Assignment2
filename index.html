Nobel Prizes (1901 - 2016)

Nobel Prizes Throughout the World

Alfred Bernhard Nobel was the founder of the Nobel prize. As the Swedish inventor of dynamite, Alfred Nobel created the Nobel prize in an attempt to alleviate the shadow of death and destruction that resided over his legacy.

The earliest Nobel Prize winners in 1901 were from France, Germany, Poland and the Netherlands. From 1901 to 2016 a total of 942 Nobel prizes have been awarded across 89 countries. 57% of these Nobels have been won by the USA (275), Germany (89), United Kingdoms (87), France (56) and Russia (31).

A majority of the awarded Nobel prizes have been won by western countries. With the exception of Russia, China is the country that has won the most Nobels on the Asia continent (13).

Notable Nobel Prize Winners

Marie Curie: First person to win two Nobels for the discovery of radioactivity (1903) and discovery of radium and polonium (1911).

Albert Einstein: Nobel prize in Physics (1921) discovering the cause of the photoelectric effect.

Alexander Fleming: Nobel in Medicine (1945) discovery of penicillin.

Watson, Crick & Wilkins: Nobel in Medicine (1962) discovery of DNA helix shape.

Martin Luthor King Jr.: Nobel in Peace (1964) work to end racial discrimination in the United States through nonviolent means.

Nobel Prizes By Category

The most awarded Nobels prizes are those of medicine (227), physics (222) and chemistry (194). In all Nobel prizes categories, the awards have been predominately awarded to men. With only 50 Nobel prizes being award to women across the history of the award. This amounts to only 5.3% of Nobel Prize winners.

Gender Imbalance

From 1901 to 1990 women made up less than 25% of all Nobel prize winners for each decade. After 1990 the portion of female winners is still disproportionate in physics, chemistry, economics and medicine. The only categories that have seen this percentage increase as of the 1990s are literature (30%) and peace (50%).

Age Distribution

There is a minimal trend in the age distribution of Nobel prize winners. The youngest ever winner was Malala Yousafzai age 17, awarded in the peace category. This Nobel is likely the reason that peace is the only category with a decreasing trend line. In contrast, the oldest winner was Leonid Hurwicz age 90 in the economics category.
